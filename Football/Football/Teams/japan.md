# Japan

## Overview
WC2026 Group F. **Final standing: 2nd (5pts — 1W 2D 0L)**
MD1: 3-1 Tunisia | MD2: 3-3 Netherlands | MD3: 1-1 Sweden
R32 fixture: vs Brazil, Jun 29.

Japan are unbeaten in 3 games and the most dangerous underdog in the tournament. The headline stat: **+3.08 Goals vs xG** — they've scored 7 goals from 3.92 xG, an overperformance that will partially correct. But their defensive structure is genuinely elite (xG/shot allowed: 0.06 — best in the dataset). Strip the variance and Japan are a real team, just not a 7-goal team.

## Style of Play

**Build-up structure**: 51.5% possession — balanced. 86.6% pass accuracy. 348 final-third passes across 3 games (116/game). Wide-heavy: left 29.2% + right 30.6% pass direction share. 34 crosses, 29.4% success. Only 7 through balls — Japan don't play balls in behind. **0 goals from 5 build-up sequences and 0 from 2 direct attacks** — all 7 goals come from transitions, individual moments, or set pieces. Not from designed patterns.

**Pressing intensity**: PPDA 12.6, press start 40.3m — mid-block. Japan sit compact and organised rather than pressing aggressively. 9 high turnovers, 2 shot-ending (22.22%) — better conversion of turnovers than most teams in dataset. 23 pressed sequences confirm passive defensive shape.

**Defensive shape**: **xG/shot faced: 0.06 — the best defensive quality restriction in the entire dataset.** Japan funnel opponents into poor positions. Only 23 shots conceded / 3 games (7.67/90). Goals vs xGA: **+1.52 unlucky** — opponents have massively overperformed their xG against Japan. The structure is genuinely elite; the 3 goals conceded are bad luck + Suzuki's limitations.

**Transition play**: Direct speed 1.75. Japan's dangerous moment is winning the ball and releasing quickly. Junya Itō (14 crosses, +3.92 On-Off) is the primary wide threat from the right. Kamada late-arriving from midfield is the primary scoring mechanism (2G/2sh, G/SoT 1.00).

## Strengths
- **xG/shot allowed: 0.06** — best defensive shape quality in the dataset
- Junya Itō: +3.92 On-Off, 14 crosses, 1G — right side is Japan's best attack channel
- Kamada: 2G/2sh, G/SoT 1.00 — both shots on target, both goals
- Shiogai: +11.83 On-Off in 7 mins — most extreme sub impact in dataset
- 1 yellow card in 3 games — near-perfect discipline (only Taniguchi)
- Unbeaten (1W 2D) — mentally strong in tight games
- 46 sustained sequences (10+ passes) — patient when needed

## Weaknesses
- **G/SoT 0.64 is massively inflated** — xG/shot is only 0.14; regression inevitable
- **xG overperformance: +3.08** — Japan have scored 3 more goals than expected; some correction coming
- Suzuki save% 72.7% — slightly below average (acceptable, not elite)
- **ALL regular starters have negative On-Off**: Kamada -3.93, Maeda -4.49, Doan -2.31, Ueda -1.14, Nakamura -2.10 — Japan's XI is weaker than their bench pattern suggests
- 0 goals from build-up or direct attacks — attacking structure doesn't create; relies on chaos
- Japan fouls heavily: 42 fouls / 3 games (14/game) — cards market risk

## On-Off paradox
Every regular starter is net-negative. Every sub is net-positive. Japan play their best football when the starters are tired and the bench comes on. This is the same structural pattern as Germany. Halftime and 60-75 min windows are when Japan become most dangerous.

## Game-State Tendencies
**When leading**: Compact mid-block. xG/shot 0.06 means even compact blocks work. Japan are hard to break down when defending a lead.
**When drawing**: Patient possession (51.5%). Itō crosses from the right. Kamada late run. Sub wave (Shiogai) changes the game.
**When trailing**: Open up. Their 3-3 vs Netherlands shows they can come from behind. But +3.08 xG overperformance means their goals are partly luck — can they do it again under pressure?

## Set Piece Profile
**Attacking set pieces**: 1 goal from 5 shots (0.40 xG) — modest set piece threat. But **headers: 5 attempts, 2 goals (40% conversion)** — extraordinary aerial clinical rate. Itō's cross delivery creates the header opportunities (14 crosses). Fast breaks: 2 attempts, 1 goal. 47 box touches across 3 games (15.7/game).

**Defending set pieces**: 13 shots conceded, 0.94 xG — **63.40% of all Japan's xGA comes from set pieces**. Open-play xGA is only 0.54 across 3 games — the most compact open-play defence in the dataset. Set pieces are Japan's one genuine structural vulnerability. Headers conceded: 9 attempts, 1 goal. 54 box touches allowed (18/game).

**Aerial duels**: Won 48.7% — the best of the four R32 teams profiled. Japan can compete in the air despite their set piece defensive weakness. 97 clearances across 3 games — the highest in the dataset; Japan defend by flooding the box.
