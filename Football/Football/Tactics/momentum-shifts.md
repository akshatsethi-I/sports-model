# Momentum Shifts

## Definition
A change in which team controls the match — territorially, tactically, or psychologically. Observable pattern: sustained pressure, high defensive lines retreating, crowd engagement changing, substitutions becoming reactive rather than planned.

## Primary triggers

### Goals
- **Early goal (0–20 min)**: Most decisive trigger. Scoring team often drops into structure; conceding team must open up. Amplifies the original tactical mismatch.
- **Equaliser**: Psychologically resets the match. The equalising team often carries momentum for 10–15 minutes afterward.
- **Late goal (75+ min)**: Can shatter a well-structured defensive block; conceding team loses defensive discipline for the final minutes.

### Structural events
- **Red card**: Immediately shifts numerical and psychological balance. 10-man teams statistically defend better in the 10–20 mins after the red (adrenaline, shape-tightening) before fatigue sets in.
- **Injury to key player**: Especially impactful when a pressing midfielder or defensive anchor is lost — the system degrades, not just the personnel.
- **Substitutions**: Tactical subs by the *winning* side can invite pressure. Reactive subs by the *losing* side signal desperation and often open space for counter-attacks.

### Environmental / game state
- **Crowd effect**: Home crowd noise after a near-miss or strong tackle can lift press intensity visibly.
- **Referee decisions**: Contentious calls can disrupt concentration for 5–10 minutes regardless of which team benefits.
- **Weather / pitch conditions**: Heavy rain, high heat or altitude affect high-press teams more than low-block teams.

## The 60–75 minute fatigue window
The most common momentum shift window in football:
- High-press teams that dominated the first hour often concede territory as legs tire
- If the score is level at 60 min, the team that has been pressing harder is statistically more likely to concede in this window
- Watch *when* and *what position* the first substitution is made — it reveals the manager's read of the match

## In prediction models
Structure scenarios around likely triggers:
1. What happens if Team A scores first vs Team B?
2. Which team's system degrades more under a red card?
3. Which team has the depth to sustain or reverse momentum via bench quality?
